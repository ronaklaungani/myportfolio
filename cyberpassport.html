Cyberpassport


Cyberpassport is an effort to:
Digitally organize the standardized cybersecurity curriculum drawn by the National Institute of Standards & Technology (NIST), US Department of Commerce
Allow accredited organizations to organize workshops in compliance to the curriculum
Allow students & cybersecurity enthusiasts to find and book these workshops.

It is a research & product development project under Dr. Li-Chiou Chen, a professor at Pace University in New York City. The project is funded through a grant sponsored by the US Department of Defense - Cyber Scholarship Program.

The Problem at Hand

The National Institute of Standards & Technology (NIST) has laid down a one-for-all curriculum (or syllabus) to standardize the education for cybersecurity in the United States. This is called the NICE framework. Accredited educational and professional institutions from around the United States adapt this curriculum to organize workshops to train students and cybersecurity enthusiasts. So we need to solve the following problems:

Breakdown the NICE framework and design a curriculum page where users can easily explore and understand it
Create a platform which allows the organizers to organize workshops and link them to the curriculum
Allow users to explore these workshops and the curriculum associated with it
Allow users to book these workshops through an online portal
Allow users to build their profile so they can add the skills attained through the workshops
Build tools to help the organizers in organizing the workshops, even for the on-ground operations.

Understanding the Data

Breaking down the curriculum

The NICE framework drawn by the NIST is a very large and complicated system of nodes. It is very important for the user to understand the taxonomies of the curriculum before making a decision about their career choices. Hence, it is really important to give users a very easy portal to explore anything and everything about the framework. But before that, let's breakdown the data. Here's how this data was shared with us:

Now. Yes, I know this is a lot to organize. But that's what makes the problem so interesting to solve. There are 3000+ rows of data per sheet provided by the NIST for the framework. The framework has four main entities, each falling under the previous one in heirarchy:
Speciality Areas Job Roles Competencies Knowledge/Skills/Abilities/Tasks (KSATs)

Here's how these entities are related to each other:

And yes, those are the colors and shapes we are going to use to show the entities throughout the design system. Now that we understand how the framework has been designed, let's see how we decided upon "the most relevant entity".

How does a user attain a Work Role?

Now the curriculum is known, the users are on the website; how do they learn and, most importantly get the certificate / badge / credit for learning these things? Workshops.
Professional and educational institutions can organize workshops, and link the curriculum to it.
The users attend this workshop, and the linked curriculum gets added to their profile.

How can one link the curriculum to a workshop?

Before we go ahead, it's really important to figure out the most relevant entity. The one users will understand the quickest and find easier to navigate with. Will the user search for workshops based on the Work Roles it covers? Or is that too broad?
After weeks of discussion with the team and weighing all the metrics, we decided that "Competency is the most relevant entity". And for the following reasons:

Most importantly - during a workshop, a Work Role is taught by covering the competencies; but not all competencies are covered during each workshop. Hence a user might cover "some" competencies of a Work Role
Competencies serve as granular "chapters" for each Work Role, but are not too granular like KSATs
This means the user can have a granular understanding of the Work Role and will approach the Work Role like students approach a course - a series of chapters
It is less complicated to use it as a filtering or sorting factor on the home page. Meaning: the user can filter the workshops based on Competencies and find the one that fits their interest.

Hence, while creating a workshop, the organizing institute can link the competencies to the workshop. And when the users attend this workshop, these linked competencies get added to their profile - where they can keep track of their progress and share their profile with potential recruiters.

How can users build their profile?

Our users will be working hard, attending hours of workshops to attain competencies that will eventually add up to a work role. To make this process more rewarding, I focused hard to gamify the whole product experience. As a part of this effort, I introduced what we named as the Career Path.
It is a section where the users can plan, follow and keep a track of their cybersecurity career. The section has three main parts:
My Work Roles: A list of all Work Roles, with a tracker for each showing the total number of competencies required and the number of competencies completed by the user.
My Focus List: This feature is expected to help our users in a major way (Usability studies are going on as I write this). We've provided a section where users can add competencies they need to focus on. Our smart algorithm then suggests workshops and organizations based on the user's focus list. The user is also notified when a new workshop related to their focus list is posted.
My Following List: Users can follow organizations to get notified whenever a new workshop is posted by that organization.

These three features help the user to have a focused approach towards what they want to achieve.

Building tools for the organizers.

Our team at Cyberpassport strongly believed and shared the enthusiasm of teaching with the organizations. Hence, we wanted to build the right tools for them so their work could be done in a much easier and proficient way. Here are few of the features we designed specifically for our organizers:

Simplified forms to create workshops and easily link competencies.
Team builder to allow the organization admin to build and manage a team that can assist in organizing as well as conducting workshops.
QR code scanner built right in their Cyberpassport mobile app so they can check-in and check-out the workshop attendees at the venue without the need of a desktop.
Manage Registration from anywhere. Add new registrations on the go with the the Cyberpassport mobile app.

Functional Requirements.

Now that we are clear about the features (from the Research section) that need to be a part of our system, let's define our functional requirements:
Organizers can create a workshop on the desktop web app & link competencies to it
Users approved by the organizers can also create workshops
Users can browse through the list of workshops based on competencies, location and time of the workshop
Users can register for the workshops online
Each user should have a unique QR code - to be scanned at the workshop venue
The system should update the user's profile with obtained competencies after every workshop
User should be able to plan their workshops under "My Career Path".

Keeping of all the functionalities in mind, I divided the product into four main sections to draw up the navigation.

For both desktop web and native mobile, we take advantage of this segmentation of functionalitites to provide a better navigation.

Crafting the Visual Language

Typography

Keep it simple, silly. I've used a simple yet a very versatile font family for the complete project: Lato. The variety of weight options, the partially curved edges make it just the right font to go with the design concepts in my mind.

Colors

The initial usability test suggested that we could drop the bright orange and use a bit more cooler color sheme. Blue suits well since it is the color for trust and has a sincere feel to it. A perfect color for an educational app. Yellow as the secondary color complements the blue and is mainly used to signify competencies throughout the system.

Iconography

Iconography for this product has been designed inspired by the simplest of ideas we encounter around us. Taking advantage of the basic human intuition, we have worked hard to make this app easy to understand and navigate. Icons, with no doubt have played the major part in this process.

Designing the Entities

Since the project involved a complex taxonomy for the data, I wanted to create disctinct visual representation for each entitity of the product. This made sure the entities and the relation between them is communicated well through the project.

Language

Language is how we communicate, how we share, how we express our intentions. It is highly important that we use this important asset in the best way possible. Language, is as important as an icon for visual design. To make sure we set the right tone for our product that matches the color scheme, we strictly followed these three foundation elements: Friendly, Precise and Informative.
Don't waste the user's time by putting a copy that does not make them feel better, is not to the point or does not provide proper feedback.
